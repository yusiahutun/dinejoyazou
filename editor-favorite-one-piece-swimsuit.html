This Editors Favorite One-Piece Swimsuit Was Designed by a Puffer Jacket Brand

In My Most Worn, Bazaar editors share the fashion items they cherish more than any others in their closets. Here, senior fashion editor Tara Gonzalez explains why a swimsuit made by an Icelandic brand known for its puffer jackets is an unexpected summer go-to.

For a long time, I was convinced Pamela Anderson had ruined one-piece swimsuits. She looked so divine and so good in her signature red suit, all hope for the rest of us seemed lost. After being raised on photos of her running across the beach on Baywatch, I found a suit similar to hers in high school—only to be left confused when I didn’t instantly transform into an earthbound goddess after slipping it on.

There’s just something about the simplicity of a one-piece swimsuit. And for most of my life, I was convinced that something was possessed only by Anderson, and that the rest of us just didn’t have it. She made a name for herself by showing skin, but she made the simple red suit look sexy because she oozed her signature confidence even while more covered-up. Chasing that subtle sensuality felt impossible to me.

Until I went to Iceland.

The Nordic region is not a place typically associated with swimwear. When I packed my bag for a trip there last December, I entirely forgot to bring a suit, despite knowing I would be visiting the famous blue lagoon near the capital of Reykjavík. Then, at 66°North’s flagship store, I picked up a one-piece that made me understand the silhouette’s appeal. The Icelandic brand first made a name for itself in 1926 with puffer coats made for the country’s search-and-rescue teams, and more recently for its popular fashion collaborations with brands like Ganni and Charlie Constantinou. It also makes a signature one-piece that stood out amongst its other offerings built for far more extreme conditions.

Courtesy of Tara Gonzalez

Me, wearing the swimsuit as a bodysuit out to dinner with a chunky cardigan and white lace maxi skirt

The Alda swimsuit by 66°North doesn’t come in the fiery red Anderson made popular. But the four colors that are available include “Elf Queen,” a light purple whose name was enough to convince me that if I wore it, I would emerge from the water with the eccentric ethereal energy of Björk. The scoop-neck design has a distinctive logo cross strap and high leg, and the suit is crafted out of a chlorine-resistant material from Italian fabric manufacturer Carvico. Despite my failure to fall in love with similar suits in the past, this one felt different.

Perhaps it was that, like everything 66°North makes, the suit was designed with purpose. I have more than a handful of bikinis and one-pieces that instantly invite wardrobe malfunctions after encountering a singular large wave. I’ve even relegated some of them to a special pile, only to be worn when I’m certain I won’t be moving much on the beach, or swimming at all. But the Alda is something I could easily do laps in, without a worry in the world. And the ease with which I could wear it gives me that confidence I’ve been searching for in similar swimsuits.

Courtesy of Tara Gonzalez

Wearing the Alda one-piece in the blue lagoon

I wore it to the blue lagoon out of necessity—I had nothing else with me—but I have since packed it on every seaside vacation or beach weekend I’ve gone on. I’ve also found myself wanting to wear it out as a bodysuit, styled with chunky cardigans in the winter and long maxi skirts in the summer. It’s the most unexpected swimwear staple I have, which I think also lies at the core of its appeal. And it’s the type of thing high-school me was desperately searching for but didn’t realize could only be found 2,737 miles away, in a place where I least expected it.

Tara Gonzalez is the Senior Fashion Editor at Harper’s Bazaar. Previously, she was the style writer at InStyle, founding commerce editor at Glamour, and fashion editor at Coveteur.
