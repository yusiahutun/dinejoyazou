Diver Xisco Grcia who survived 60 hours in flooded cave

A diver has revealed the horror of being stuck alone in an underground tunnel for 60 hours with only a carbon-dioxide heavy air pocket to help him breathe.

Xisco Gràcia was exploring the intricate Sa Piqueta cave system under the waters of Mallorca with his friend Guillem Mascaró when disaster struck.

An hour into the dive on April 15, the pair split up so geology teacher Gràcia could take samples and Mascaró could peruse the cave.

But when they swam into each other unexpectedly in Sa Piqueta's labyrinthine chambers, they stirred silt up from the ground and lost their guideline - the wire which leads them back to the surface.

'We can only guess some rocks had fallen on it,' Gràcia told the BBC. 'We spent a precious hour trying to find it by touch, but without success.'

It is then father-of-two Gràcia took the courageous decision to send Mascaró up to find help while he survived breathing the air from an underwater air pocket.

Gràcia said: 'He was skinnier than me and needed less air for breathing. I was also more experienced at breathing cave air, which has higher carbon dioxide levels.

'Guillem was reluctant to leave me on my own but we knew it was our only chance.'

Gràcia immediately took off most of his equipment and soon learned the water near the surface of the chamber, which had a 12m gap between the water surface and the ceiling, was undrinkable.

He pulled himself out of the water and onto a rock to rest but with only one working torch light, he accepted he would be spending the majority of his time in total darkness.

At first he thought he would be rescued within eight hours but soon began to lose hope, saying: 'Guillem has got lost and died and no-one knows I am down here.'

With the cave's oxygen at five per cent, compared to the 0.04% in the earth's atmosphere, he got severe headaches and found it impossible to sleep.

After what felt like days, he reached his darkest moment. With his torchlight almost spent, he swam to where he left his gear to get his knife.

He said: 'I wanted to have it as a last resort if I needed to choose whether to die quickly or slowly.'

Then, a miracle. He thought he was hallucinating when he saw a diver's light emerging from the water but soon recognised his old friend Bernat Clamor.

'I jumped in to the water and embraced him. He was asking me how I was, and telling me that he had been afraid I had died.'

Gràcia learned that Mascaró had raised the alarm but poor visibility hindered the rescue.

At first Gracia (centre) thought he would be rescued within eight hours but soon began to lose hope

It took eight hours for Gràcia to be slowly guided out of the cave as he was given oxygen-enriched air. He emerged on April 17, 60 hours after going in.

'You have to be able to control your emotions with diving,' he said of his ordeal. 'But the next day I watched coverage of the big rescue operation on TV and I cried.'

A month after his escape, Gràcia returned to Sa Piqueta and has even visited the chamber where he was stranded.
