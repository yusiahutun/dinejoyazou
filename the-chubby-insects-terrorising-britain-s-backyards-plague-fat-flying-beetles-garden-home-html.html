The chubby insects terrorising Britain's backyards: The plague of fat, flying beetles found in your

They spend four years fattening themselves up underground. So when they finally emerge into the May or June sunshine, they are rather disorientated – and maybe a little angry.

Little wonder, then, that gardeners across the South-East have reported a plague of giant flying cockchafer beetles terrorising them in their own back yards.

The insects, which grow to more than an inch long and make a loud whirring noise in flight, have been 'dive-bombing' residents.

On Twitter, Daniel Ott wrote: 'Urgh, it's cockchafer (May bug) time! They're so big, stupid & creepy. Sound horrendous as they dive-bomb the backdoor cos of the light!'

The insects (pictured), which grow to more than an inch long and make a loud whirring noise in flight, have been 'dive-bombing' residents

Another user, Paige Hunter, said a friend had fallen downstairs after being 'attacked' by one. And Samantha Scott, 37, of Norwich, said: 'I've never seen such a hideous and loud little beastie before – it came into my bedroom and buzzed around for ages. I ended up sleeping in the lounge.'

In reality, cockchafers are harmless to humans but their voracious habitats have made them a deadly enemy to farmers over the centuries.

In the Middle Ages the French would eat them in stews or fried with butter, while in Germany they were coated in sugar and eaten as sweets.

Modern pesticides wiped them out in their millions but, with new controls on the use of insecticides, numbers have begun to rise again.

Nature, however, appears to be the best control.

Known as May or June flies because of the time of year they emerge, the beetles have a short adult life-cycle that runs for five or six weeks and most have died out by the end of July.

In reality, cockchafers are harmless to humans but their voracious habitats have made them a deadly enemy to farmers over the centuries

Females will lay up to 80 eggs about three-quarters of an inch underground, where the grubs will hatch after six weeks and feed on grass roots for three to four years before emerging as adults.

Paul Bates, who runs a pest control firm, said that while the beetles are not a public health risk, encountering one could be quite an alarming experience. 'Sometimes they can cause a problem by coming down a chimney and scaring the homeowner,' he said.

'They make a very loud noise when flying which can be a little scary for people, but they pose no danger to health. They don't sting.'
