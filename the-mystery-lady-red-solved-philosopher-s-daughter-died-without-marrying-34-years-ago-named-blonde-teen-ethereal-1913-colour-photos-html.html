Identity of mysterious woman from 1913 colour photos revealed

A teenage girl, known only as ‘Christina’, who appeared in a set of 102-year-old colour portraits has been identified as Christina Elizabeth Frances Bevan, a woman who died without marrying in 1981 at the age of 84.

Scholars have been puzzling over the ethereal strawberry blonde, who was captured by photographer Mervyn O'Gorman in a series of dreamlike photos taken in Lulworth Cove, Dorset, in 1913, and for years was thought to be his daughter.

Now a retired engineer has named Christina, a family friend of Mr O'Gorman and the daughter of a prominent philosopher at King's College London, who sparked curiosity when the cache of dreamlike colour pictures was discovered and put on display.

Her full name is now revealed to have been Christina Elizabeth Frances Bevan, and she died in 1981 when she was 84, with no listed husband or children

Christina (centre) would have been 16 when the photos were taken in Dorset in 1913, pictured with her mother and sister in a previously unseen stereoscopic slide belonging to Mr O'Gorman - and recently dug out by a retired technician

The captivating images - among the oldest surviving photos in history - are owned by the Royal Photographic Society, and are currently on display at the National Media Museum, Bradford.

Upon seeing them, many scholars and amateur historians were keen to identify the young beauty, then 16, who was one of the first people to be photographed in colour; but rather than gained fame, vanished from records.

The photos, labelled only 'Christina', were taken using the autochrome process by Mr O'Gorman - a scientist known to have been an early pioneer of the technique.

Initially, the teen was presumed to be Mr O'Gorman's daughter, but further research showed that he had never had children, meaning her true identity remained a mystery, until now.

As a result of seeing the images, Stephen Riddle, a retired technician, 73, contacted National Media Museum curator Colin Harding to say he had a set of stereoscopic slides by Mr O’Gorman, which had been passed to him by his father-in-law.

The slides feature colour autochrome pictures taken around the same time at the originals not previously seen by anyone at the museum, which - crucially - had captions referring to 'Edwyn and Daisy Bevan', along with ‘the children’, Anne and Christina.

Christina (right) was initially presumed to be the daughter of Mr O'Gorman (left), an engineer and photographer, who was 43 when he shot the dreamlike photos - but was in fact was the daughter of Mr O'Gorman's family friend 

The newly unearthed slides (pictured), taken by Mr O'Gorman at the same time as the original portraits of Christina, crucially had captions referring to 'Daisy Bevan' (left) - Christina's mother - and her ‘children’, Anne (right) and Christina (centre) 'watching the birds'

Christina's sister, pictured in another slide with Christina and her mother at the same beach in West Lulworth, was Anne Cornelia Favell Bevan, who was born in 1898 and died in 1983 - and bore two children 

Her mother, pictured in the same slide as previous, was born in 1870, and was formally the Hon. Mary Waldegrave. She married Christina's father Edwyn Bevan on April 25, 1896, and thus became Mary Bevan. She was known by family and friends as 'Daisy'

In another previously unseen slide, Christina’s sister Anne on a horse outside their home, captioned, 'Anne on Victor with a Chelsea Pensioner, Edwyn and Daisy outside 6 Chelsea Embankment, 1913'

The photos were taken in various locations including the beach at West Lulworth and outside an address in Chelsea Embankment - and the Bevan family appear to have been friends with the O'Gorman family, who were on a trip together. 

As Mr Harding tells MailOnline, Christina was born in Harrow on March 8, 1897, and died in Aylesbury in 1981 at the age of 84 - although little else is known about her.

She was the daughter of Edwyn and Mary Bevan. Her father, Edwyn Robert Bevan, was born in 1870 and died in 1943 and was a prominent philosopher, writer on comparative religions and lecturer in Hellenistic Studies at King’s College, London.

Her mother, formally the Hon. Mary Waldegrave, was born in 1870, married Edwyn on April 25, 1896, and thus became Mary Bevan. She was known by family and friends as 'Daisy', and it is not known when she died. 

Mr and Mrs Bevan had two daughters – Anne Cornelia Favell Bevan, who was born in 1898 and died in 1983, and Christina.

Mr Harding explains: 'The Bevan family lived at no. 6 Chelsea Embankment – just a two minute walk from the O’Gorman’s home at 21 Embankment Gardens. 

'The precise relationship between the two families still needs to be explored – perhaps Mr Bevan and Mr O'Gorman were members of the same club, or perhaps they shared a mutual interest in automobiles. Perhaps Mr O’Gorman’s wife, Florence, and Mrs Bevan were friends.

'Whatever the link, both families were clearly on friendly, first name terms. 

'Certainly, the friendship was sufficient for Mr O'Gorman to accompany Mrs Bevan and her two daughters on a trip to Lulworth Cove in August 1913, where he took portraits of Christina.'

He adds: 'We are very grateful to Mr Riddle for contacting us and it was a genuine thrill to see these images. After all the recent attention Christina had been getting I hoped they would give us sufficient clues to finally confirm her identity.' 

According to The Peerage, Christina never married or had children. 

Her sister Anne, however, married a Charles Craven Sanders and had two children; Florence, who died at the age of 15 in 1943, and Robert, who was born in 1931 and has no death date listed.

As for Mr O'Gorman, who took the revolutionary portraits of Christina, he was born in Brighton is 1871 and studied science at University College, Dubin. He later worked in electrical engineering and had a penchant for cars, eventually being crowned vice president of the Royal Automobile Club.

The talented entrepreneur married Florence Rasch in 1897, and during the first World War, he became a lieutenant-colonel in the RFC.

When he died in 1958 at the age of 87, Mr O'Gorman's obituary described him as 'a man of agile mind and Hibernian eloquence'.

Christina wore red in every shot he took of her at the beach, probably at the request of Mr O'Gorman, since the vibrant colour captured particularly well using the autochrome process.

Patented in 1903, it involved using glass plates covered in microscopic grains of potato starch to filter pictures with dye.

Before Mr Harding uncovered Christina's identity, he said: 'Ultimately, who Christina was isn’t important. O’Gorman’s portraits retain their timeless impact.'
